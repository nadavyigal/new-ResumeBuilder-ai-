The Retention Problem

Job seekers churn once hired. That's the nature of the category. The question is whether Resumely stays a one-shot tool or builds a reason to return. What's the hook?

A

Accept it — optimise for the sprint, not retention

Don't fight the episodic nature. Design for a 4–8 week intense job search period. Price accordingly (credit packs are already designed for this). Revenue comes from the next cohort of job seekers, not repeat users.

Model: High volume, transactional. Growth = constant new users entering job search. Credit packs capture the value. No need to retain post-hire.

Honest

  • Matches how users actually behave
  • No fake retention hooks that feel spammy
  • Focus stays on the core value (getting hired)

Business risk

  • LTV is capped at one job search
  • Growth depends on constant new user acquisition
  • No compounding user base
B

Career layer — resume stays alive after the job

Position Resumely as a living career document, not just a job-application tool. "Keep your resume current" nudges every 3–6 months. Annual subscription unlocks ongoing tracking, LinkedIn sync, promotion prep.

Model: Career OS. Annual subscription justified by "always-ready resume." Notification at 3 months: "You've been at your new job 3 months — update your resume while it's fresh."

Higher LTV

  • Annual subscriber stays even between job searches
  • Defensible moat — resume history, templates, career data
  • Works with LinkedIn integration (future)

Hard to justify

  • Users won't pay monthly when not job hunting
  • Requires building beyond the core use case
  • Risk of becoming a bloated product
C

Referral engine — users bring the next users

When a user exports their resume, prompt them to share with a job-hunting friend. "Know someone job hunting? Give them a free export." Turns every happy user into an acquisition channel before they churn.

Model: Referral at the moment of value (post-export). One happy user → one new install. The churn is fine if each user generates 0.5–1 new user before leaving.

Solves the growth problem

  • Referral loops compound — each cohort seeds the next
  • No extra retention needed — just capture value before churn
  • Job hunting is social — "my friend used this" is trusted

Requires incentive

  • Needs a referral credit system to build
  • Referral loops are hard to get right on first attempt
